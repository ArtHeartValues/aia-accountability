When the garden looks dormant
Stillness ¬∑ Apparent endings ¬∑ Winter
‚ñæ

Dormancy and ending look almost identical from the outside. The same stillness. The same absence of visible green. The same silence that feels like loss.

The difference is underground. Roots don't stop during winter ‚Äî they contract, conserve, hold. They are doing something, even when nothing shows. What looks like stopping is often the beginning of the next season's growth.

You don't have to convince yourself that spring is coming. You just have to resist the urge to pull things up while they are resting.

The paradox of dormancy: the most important tending is to do less. The hardest practice is trusting what you cannot see.

Experienced gardeners learn to read dormancy differently than the untrained eye does. They know the difference between something resting and something gone. That knowledge comes slowly, from staying close long enough to notice.

One thing for dormant seasons: stop pulling things up. Let what's resting, rest. That is the whole practice.
When you've been tending faithfully and see nothing change
Good effort ¬∑ Slow results ¬∑ Exhaustion
‚ñæ

There is a particular ache in tending faithfully and seeing nothing shift. You show up. You water. You wait. The ground stays dry-looking. This is one of the hardest places in any garden ‚Äî and in being human.

What gardeners learn over time: some of what you plant takes years to establish. The tree you placed in the ground last spring is spending its whole first year on roots, not height. From the outside, nothing happened. From the inside, everything is being built.

The paradox of invisible growth: faithfulness that looks like failure. Effort that registers nowhere you can see it. And yet the accounting is happening, underground, on a schedule you didn't set.

Your showing up is not wasted because you cannot see it yet. The garden does not forget what you have given it. It is holding it, transforming it, using it in ways that will only become visible later ‚Äî sometimes much later than feels fair.

One thing for this season: acknowledge what you have already done. Not to perform it ‚Äî just to see it clearly. It counts, whether or not it shows yet.
When the garden needs more than you have
Depletion ¬∑ Overflow ¬∑ Real limits
‚ñæ

No gardener has unlimited water. No soil gives endlessly without being replenished. Real interdependence includes knowing when you need to receive tending yourself ‚Äî not just provide it.

The gardener who never rests, never lets someone else carry the watering can ‚Äî that gardener eventually cannot tend anything. Your depletion is not a character flaw. It is a signal that tending is needed, for you.

The paradox of care: the more you love the garden, the more you may ignore your own exhaustion. And the more you ignore it, the less you can actually offer the things you love.

What the garden needs most right now might be for you to put down the tools for a while. Gardens are more resilient than fear tells you. They can hold themselves for an afternoon. They have been doing it for longer than you've been watching.

One thing: name one person or one thing that has tended you lately. If you cannot name one ‚Äî that is important information about what this season is asking for.
When something you tended didn't survive
Loss ¬∑ Grief ¬∑ What remains
‚ñæ

There is a specific grief in losing something you tended well. Not just the loss of the thing, but the disorientation of your care not being enough. Of doing everything right and having it not matter in the way you needed it to.

This grief is not the same as regret. You can grieve something that was not your fault, that no one could have prevented, that simply could not survive in this soil at this time. The love you gave it was real. That does not stop being true because the outcome was not what you hoped.

The paradox of grief in the garden: what is lost was real, and what remains is also real. Both are true at the same time. The loss does not negate what was grown. What was grown does not cancel the loss.

Beginning again ‚Äî when you are ready, if you are ever ready ‚Äî does not mean forgetting. It means finding out what remains after loss. And what remains is usually more than grief tells you in the early days.

The grief is appropriate. You loved something. That doesn't need to be resolved. It needs to be held until it can be carried.
When something grows that surprises you
Emergence ¬∑ Unexpected bloom ¬∑ Disorientation
‚ñæ

Not all gardeners know what to do when things actually bloom. Sometimes growth arrives and it is ‚Äî disorienting. You don't trust it. You wait for it to end. You find reasons it doesn't count or won't last.

Growth is allowed to be real even when you didn't expect it. What is blooming does not need you to explain how it happened, or argue it out of existence, or hold it at a distance until you've decided it's safe.

The paradox of unexpected bloom: the part of you that protected you through the hard seasons can become the part that won't let you receive the good ones. The same vigilance that kept you safe can keep out the light.

You are allowed to receive what your garden produces. Harvest is not greed. It is completing the cycle. The garden grew this for you. You are allowed to take it.

One thing: name one thing that has grown, even small. Say it somewhere ‚Äî out loud if you can. Let it be real for one minute before you qualify it.
When you don't know where to begin
Uncertainty ¬∑ No map ¬∑ First season
‚ñæ

Most gardening knowledge comes from watching ‚Äî not reading. From staying with something long enough to notice what it responds to. The novice and the master share this: both are learning to see what is actually there.

You do not need to understand the whole garden before you begin. You need to stay close enough to notice when something changes. That is the whole skill. Proximity and patience.

The paradox of beginning: you cannot know what the garden needs before you have tended it. But you cannot tend it without beginning before you know. You have to start in uncertainty. There is no other way in.

If you do not know what to do right now, the first tending is simply this: keep showing up. The garden teaches you what it needs when you stay close enough to hear. It will tell you. It always does, to those who stay.

One thing: show up tomorrow. Don't decide today what tomorrow needs. Just agree to come back. That is enough for now.
