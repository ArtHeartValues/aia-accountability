When the garden looks dead ‚ñæ
Dormancy ¬∑ Winter ¬∑ Apparent failure

Most gardeners don't know that dormancy and death look almost identical from the outside. The same stillness. The same absence of visible green. The same silence that feels like ending.

The difference is underground. Roots don't stop during winter ‚Äî they contract, conserve, hold. They are doing something, even when nothing shows. What looks like giving up is often the beginning of the next season's growth.

You don't have to convince yourself that spring is coming. You just have to not pull the plant up while it's resting.

One thing for dormant seasons: stop pulling things up. Let what's resting, rest. That's the whole practice.

Exhaustion ¬∑ Good effort ¬∑ Slow results

There is a particular grief in tending faithfully and seeing nothing change. You show up. You water. You wait. The ground stays dry-looking. This is one of the hardest places in gardening ‚Äî and in being human.

Experienced gardeners know: some of what you plant takes years to establish. The tree you put in the ground last spring is spending its whole first year on roots, not height. From the outside, nothing happened. From the inside, everything is being built.

Your faithfulness is not wasted because you can't see it yet. The accounting happens underground, on a schedule you don't set.

One thing for this: acknowledge what you've already done. Not to perform it ‚Äî just to see it clearly. It counts.

Not all gardeners know what to do when things actually grow. Sometimes abundance arrives and it's ‚Äî disorienting. You don't trust it. You wait for it to die. You find reasons it doesn't count.

Most gardening knowledge comes from watching ‚Äî not reading. From sitting with something long enough to notice what it responds to. The novice gardener and the master gardener share this: both are learning to see what's actually there.
